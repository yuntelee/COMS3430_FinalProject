Final Project: Nature → Feed-Imprinted Markov Music

For our final project we built a browser-based generative music system that takes any nature recording (e.g. birds chirping, rain, ocean waves, wind, forest sounds, or any inputted audio file) and uses it as the raw material for an evolving musical composition. The core idea was that the character of the natural sound should shape everything about the generated music: its rhythm, pitch, timbre, and texture. We wanted it to feel less like "music playing over a nature recording" and more like the nature recording slowly transforming into music.

For audio feature extraction, we ran a full spectral analysis on every frame of the uploaded file, computing RMS energy, spectral centroid, spectral flatness (noisiness vs. tonality of a sound), and dominant pitch via FFT peak detection. Because of this, longer sounds take longer to analyze. From these frame-level features we then detected onsets: moments where the RMS rises sharply above the local average. Each onset becomes a gesture, or a snapshot of the sound at that moment encoded with its start pitch, mid pitch, end pitch, contour direction, duration, inter-onset gap, amplitude, brightness, noisiness, attack speed, and the original time offset.

We then trained four separate Markov chains on the sequence of gestures extracted from the recording: one for pitch states (the full multi-attribute encoding), one for rhythmic gaps (quantized to musical beat subdivisions), one for texture classes (combinations of density, brightness, and shape like "dense-bright-chirp" or "sparse-warm-sustain"), and one for bass scale degrees derived from the recording's estimated tonal center. This was a direct extension of the first-order Markov chains we covered in class. The key difference between these is that each state carries a whole vector of attributes rather than just a single pitch value. Transitions between states are weighted by how often they appeared in the original recording, and the entropy slider controls a temperature parameter that makes the chain more or less likely to deviate from the learned transitions.

As for synthesis, we layered several WebAudio voices on top of each other. The main melodic voice plays pentatonic-quantized tones using paired oscillators (a sine and a slightly detuned triangle) with note durations snapped to musical beat subdivisions derived from the tempo slider. A harmonic pad layer uses triangle oscillators tuned to chord tones below the melody. A chirp resynthesis layer plays shorter oscillator bursts with pitch contours loosely following the feed gesture, especially useful for recreating bird/forest feed sounds to ensure that all attributes of the sound are accounted for. A granular layer samples short grains directly from the original recording at the detected onset offsets, so the actual audio of the nature sound shows through at key moments. Finally, a bass layer plays sine-wave notes at scale degrees inferred from the recording's root pitch class, with an octave sub-oscillator for weight.

The sliders give real-time control over all of this. Entropy adjusts the Markov chain temperature and adds timing jitter to the note spacing. For example, low entropy produces tight, repeating patterns while high entropy produces loose, unpredictable ones. The complexity slider ("Original ↔ Melodic") shifts the oscillator waveform from triangle toward sine, moves the melody into higher octaves, and lengthens note durations from gesture-length to phrase-length. Tempo directly controls the beat unit that all note durations and gaps are quantized to. Density gates how many voices fire per beat. We then have the FM slider. This was originally wired to FM modulation depth, which we ultimately removed because it was generating "alien" like noises was repurposed to control filter brightness across all active voices, so it now functions as a timbre/tone control. The synth, bass, and drums sliders independently scale the gain of those layers.

For the visualization, we built three real-time diagrams. We added a frequency bar graph, a scrolling spectrogram, and an evolution curve that shows how far the piece has progressed from its starting state. We also display a live entropy graph that tracks how the randomness parameter has been moving over time, which is color-coded based on average entropy values.

Some issues that we ran into included getting the gain staging right across the full signal chain (generatedBus → dryGain/wetGain → musicGain → masterGain), because small changes in one gain node compound with all the others and it was easy for us to end up with either clipping or barely audible noises. Envelope design was also tricky, as we encountered a lot of clipping during testing, but ultimately switching to linearRampToValueAtTime helped with most of the clipping. Additionally, the FM synthesis we wanted to implement produced metallic, alien-like sounds at the modulation depths we needed, so we removed it from the chirp layer and repurposed to timbre control rather than try to tame it further.

Overall, we're pleased with the result of this project. Uploading a bird or nature recording and hearing those specific chirp rhythms and pitches gradually emerge as a melody that still feels connected to the source is satisfying for practicing mindfulness and meditation. If we had more time, we'd explore longer Markov chain orders so the generated sequences have more large-scale structure, and potentially add a proper reverb impulse response instead of the feed-derived convolver approximation we ended up with.
